# Home Base — The B2B Unlock

*Investor pitch summary*

---

## The one-liner

**Home Base turns trusted accountants into a zero-cost acquisition channel for property
investors, then monetises those high-intent investors by routing them to the contractors,
buyers agents, and agents who want them.** A marketplace wearing a tax tool's clothes.

---

## The problem

Australia has ~2.2M property investors ([PropTrack / Terri Scheer Investor Report
2025](https://www.realestate.com.au/insights/proptrack-terri-scheer-investor-report-2025/)).
Their financial admin is painful and high-stakes:
classifying renovation spend against ATO rules (deductible vs capital vs depreciable), tracking
expenses across properties, and getting it into their accountant's system at tax time. Existing
tools (TaxTank, The Property Accountant) sell *subscriptions* to solve this — a fragmented,
crowded, low-margin SaaS race.

Meanwhile, the businesses that desperately want these investors — contractors chasing renovation
work, buyers agents chasing the next purchase, agents chasing the next listing — pay through the
nose for **cold, low-quality leads**. hipages charges tradies $200–600/mo plus $25–80 per
form-fill lead they win 1-in-5 times. Property-investor leads sell for $70–150 a head.

Nobody connects the two. The tax tools have the investors but only sell software. The lead
platforms sell leads but have no context.

## The insight

The accountant is not the customer — **the accountant is the distribution.**

Accountants onboard their investor clients onto Home Base *for free*, because the tool saves the
accountant hours at tax time (AI reno classification grounded in actual ATO rulings, plus
one-click Xero journal posting). The investor stays because the tool is genuinely useful to *them*
— it builds a "property passport": a living record of every property's renovation history, cost
base, depreciation, and tax position.

That gives us something no lead platform has: **engaged investors with deep, structured context.**
We don't sell a cold form-fill. We sell *"this investor is planning a scoped $40k bathroom
renovation on this specific property, with this budget."* That is a 10× lead. It commands a premium
or a success fee instead of a per-lead skim.

## The business model

A two-sided marketplace where one B2B segment funds acquisition and another pays the revenue:

| Side | Role | Economics |
|------|------|-----------|
| **Accountants / brokers** | Acquisition channel | Free tool + **revenue share** — aligned partner, not exploited funnel |
| **Investors** | The asset / demand | Free; the passport is the retention hook |
| **Contractors** | Revenue (Phase 1) | Pay for scoped, high-intent renovation leads / success fee |
| **Buyers agents & agents** | Revenue (Phase 2) | Pay for portfolio-growth and listing intent at the transaction moment |
| **Community / UGC** | Engagement + 3rd channel | Investors share portfolios, tradies & advice; **home-log marketplace** (Phase 2) takes a fee on sold logs / paid unlocks |

Monetisation is **investor-initiated and opt-in** — the investor *pulls* quotes; we never push
their identity. This is the line between a valued feature and a creepy data broker, and it's the
thing that keeps the accountant channel open.

## The community layer (and why it's not a distraction)

The flywheel above has one weakness: **intent events are infrequent** — most investors renovate or
transact only occasionally, so between events there's little reason to return. A community layer
fixes exactly that, and three things make it work here that don't work for a greenfield social app:

**Seeded content from day one.** Every onboarded investor already builds a passport — a structured
record of what they did to a property, what it cost, and which tradies they used. So the network
isn't a cold, empty feed; it's populated by the core product as a by-product. Investors can opt to
make a portfolio (or a single property's "home log") **discoverable**, follow others renovating
similar properties, and ask for advice.

**This is content no one else has.** Houzz has inspiration photos; forums (PropertyChat, Reddit)
have text. Nobody has *real renovations with real cost data and the actual tradies behind them.*
"I want a Hamptons reno on a 1920s Federation home — show me others who did it, what it cost, and
who they used" is a query only Home Base can answer. It's Houzz with the financials and the
contractor attached.

**It compounds the marketplace.** Peer attribution and reviews of tradies ("3 investors in your
suburb used this builder") are a *trust layer* that makes contractor leads convert far better than a
cold hipages profile — directly lifting the price we can charge in Phase 1. Sharing tradies is the
social act that feeds the revenue engine.

**New monetisation: the home-log marketplace.** Because the passport is already a transferable
artifact, it can be *sold or unlocked*. An investor researching a project can pay to see the full
costed breakdown of a comparable renovation that aligns with what they want to achieve; the original
owner shares in that fee. The home log becomes both inspiration and a tradeable asset.

**The discipline:** this is the *engagement and trust layer of the existing product*, not a second
product. We ship the lightweight version first (discoverable passports + tradie attribution +
follow/save), and only build heavier social and the home-log marketplace once engagement is proven.
Sharing is **granular and opt-in** — show the story, the materials, the tradies and cost *ranges*
without forcing exact dollar figures or identity. Privacy is the gate, not an afterthought.

## Unit economics — *to be validated*

> The following are the levers, not yet measured numbers. Every figure below is an assumption the
> concierge test (see end of doc) and Phase 0/1 are designed to replace with real data. Stated here
> so the model is legible and the validation targets are explicit.

**Acquisition (the channel advantage)**
- **CAC per investor:** ~$0 direct — onboarded by the accountant, not bought. Real cost is the
  accountant's incentive (revenue share) + onboarding effort. *Target: validate that 1 accountant
  onboards ≥10 clients.*
- **Investors per accountant:** the multiplier that makes the whole model work. *To measure in
  Phase 0.*

**Monetisation (the revenue side)**
- **Renovation lead value:** benchmark is hipages ($25–80/lead, ~1-in-5 win) and property leads
  ($70–150). Our thesis: a *scoped, context-rich* reno lead commands a premium or a success fee on
  job value. *Target: confirm contractor willingness-to-pay in the concierge test.*
- **Intent events per investor per year:** renovations (frequent) >> purchases >> sales (rare). The
  blended frequency sets revenue-per-investor. *To measure once the marketplace is live.*
- **Take rate model:** per-lead fee vs. success fee (% of reno value) vs. subscription for
  providers — to be chosen based on what contractors actually accept.
- **Home-log marketplace fee:** take on sold/unlocked logs, shared with the original owner. *New
  per-investor revenue line, validated in Phase 2.*
- **Organic / UGC channel:** community content lowers *blended* CAC over time and de-risks reliance
  on the accountant funnel. *Target: measure share of signups arriving organically once community is
  live.*

**The equation that has to clear**

> (Investors per accountant) × (intent events / investor / yr) × (revenue / event) − (accountant
> rev-share + ops) **> 0, and ideally an order of magnitude above CAC.**

The point of staging the build is to fill in each variable cheaply before betting on the next. We do
not build the marketplace until the *revenue / event* term is proven.

## Why we win (the moat)

- **Zero-CAC distribution.** Consumer property apps die on customer acquisition cost. We get the
  investor side near-free through a *trusted intermediary* — the single hardest side of a two-sided
  market, handed to us.
- **Qualification context = pricing power.** Our leads convert far better than hipages form-fills
  because we know the property, the scope, the budget, the tax position, and the transaction intent.
- **The passport as a network asset.** When a property sells, its full history transfers with it —
  a handover moment no competitor owns, and a natural wedge into agents and conveyancers.
- **Network effects + a data moat.** The community turns every investor into content and every
  tradie into a reviewed, attributed node. That UGC is an organic acquisition channel (reducing
  dependence on the accountant funnel) and a growing corpus of real costed renovations that pure-SaaS
  incumbents (TaxTank, The Property Accountant) structurally cannot replicate.

## Why now

- AI makes defensible, ATO-ruling-grounded tax classification possible for the first time — the
  exact ambiguous call (deductible vs capital) that accountants hate making manually.
- Deep accounting integrations (live Xero journal posting) are now table stakes and we already have
  them built.
- Australian property investors are more active and more leveraged than ever; renovation and
  transaction activity is high.

## What's already built (de-risking the bet)

This is not a slideware concept. The platform already has:

- **Deep Xero integration** — multi-line manual journal posting with per-property tracking, not
  CSV export.
- **AI tax classification** — RAG grounded in real ATO rulings (TR 97/23, TR 2021/5…) with
  citations and confidence scores.
- **Stamp duty across all 8 jurisdictions**, loan/ROI math, rental tracking.
- **A sharing/permission model** that already lets investors invite their accountant.
- **The property passport** — tokenised, transferable property records.

The remaining build is the marketplace surface (a contractor portal, intent capture, lead routing)
— not the hard financial plumbing.

## Go-to-market

We already have a **warm channel into accountants and brokers** — the hard side of the market.
Sequence:

1. **Phase 0 — Activate the channel.** Give accountants a roster view + bulk tax-pack export so the
   tool saves them real hours and they push it to their book.
2. **Phase 1 — Monetise renovations + light community.** The densest, most frequent intent signal.
   Investors request quotes; contractors pay for context-rich leads. Ship discoverable passports +
   tradie attribution alongside it — peer trust lifts lead conversion and gives investors a reason to
   return between events.
3. **Phase 2 — Own the transaction + the home-log marketplace.** Passport-as-listing-dossier for
   agents and conveyancers; buyers-agent intros on portfolio growth; investors buy/unlock comparable
   home logs for a fee shared with the original owner.

## The risks (and how we handle them)

- **Two-sided cold start** → the accountant channel solves the investor side; we hand-seed
  contractor supply early.
- **Accountant trust** → opt-in, investor-pull monetisation + revenue share keeps the channel
  aligned.
- **Intent frequency** → renovations are frequent and already core to the product; we monetise
  those first, not rare transactions.
- **AI liability** → positioned as decision support the accountant reviews, never automated filing.
- **Community cold-start & focus creep** → we don't build a greenfield social network; the passport
  seeds content from day one, and we ship the *lightweight* sharing layer first. Social is the
  engagement layer of the existing product, not a second product competing for the team's attention.
- **Investor privacy / willingness to share** → sharing is granular and opt-in; investors can share
  the story, materials and tradies with cost *ranges* (not exact figures or identity). Reputation,
  anti-spam and review integrity are designed in, not retrofitted.

## The cheapest proof point (next 2 weeks, not next quarter)

Before building the marketplace, we concierge-test the core assumption: onboard ~5 investors with
planned renovations through the accountant channel, hand-match them to contractors with full
context, and confirm a contractor will *pay* for that lead. If they won't, we've lost a week. If
they will, we have our price point and the entire monetisation thesis is validated — and we ask the
channel accountants directly whether they'd offer it to their clients for a cut.
